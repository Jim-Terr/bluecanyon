American singer-songwriter hopes to bring international recognition “home”


Questions?: bluecanyon2@newmexico.com or 505-426-6612

Jim Terr has been writing songs since at least 1972, when he was drafted into the “Sweetgrass” bluegrass band in San Francisco. He is a native of Las Vegas, New Mexico, the “original” Las Vegas, founded in 1835 and a very popular TV and movie location.  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Las_Vegas,_New_Mexico

By 1988, when he self-published his well-reviewed book of original song lyrics, You Got to be Stupid to Sing Country Music, http://bluecanyonproductions.com/stupid.html  and performed an original song in the Roger Vadim remake of his 1956 Brigitte Bardot film And God Created Woman, http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0092559/  he was well on his way to the almost 90 song titles he had registered with BMI (the performance rights organization), and airplay in over 25 countries, as of 2002.  (He now estimates his songs as totaling at least 1,000).

Terr has continued to garner fans and rave reviews for his earthy-but-quirky writing and singing style, mostly from Europe as well as from two of the most successful US country music songwriters, Tom T. Hall and Bobby Braddock (Braddock is one of the most successful hit country songwriters of all time – for every one of the past five decades!)**

Well into his 60s, Terr does not aspire to be an adored stage performer, but continues to hone his craft with one aim – writing songs for hit artists – as his two greatest idols and admirers above have done so consistently. Terr notes that the publishing rights on a single successful country song (as opposed to the recording) can generate over a million dollars over its lifetime, and he’s got upwards of a hundred (10% of his total) that he feels are of that quality.

“I’ve gotten some feedback that some of my songs are too peculiar, too particular, too ‘old style country’ for the current country market, but I think things will get more ‘traditional,’ as they have many times before in country music.”  Country music is still by far the leading single US radio format.

An upcoming feature film called The Pretext ( www.pretextfilm.com ) will feature many of Terr’s songs and performances, and other feature films featuring his best songs are also in the works. The Pretext film originated as a record of Terr’s actual run in a US presidential primary in 2012, and evolved into a murder mystery, with satirical references to current US president Trump. Terr’s political satire songs have been featured on scores of major US radio and TV networks.  

The film includes cameo appearances by several American celebrities, including Suhail Dabbach of “The Hurt Locker”, the 2008 Oscar “Best Picture”  https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lA1bgUYjJ0I , Valerie Plame Wilson https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valerie_Plame, Scott Simon https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Simon , Dave Barry https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Barry , James Fallows https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Fallows , and Philip Proctor https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Proctor .

An international investment group called The Music Investors’ Group  www.MusicInvestorsGroup.com  holds much of the publishing rights to Terr’s original material, and after several years of behind-the-scenes struggles (which neither Terr nor the Group has commented on), feels that the time is right to aggressively push Terr’s material in the US – in country music capital Nashville in particular. The music group website has several articles explaining the tremendous revenue streams for publishing rights to songs recorded by major artists or used in films, TV and commercials. ( Terr is also a recognized writer of advertising jingles, with several recognizable ones to his credit; see www.TheSongwriter.us ).

British journalist and Nashville veteran Tony Byworth, co-editor of The BILLBOARD ENCYCLOPEDIA OF COUNTRY MUSIC, has followed Terr’s career for many years and twelve self-published albums (not counting limited-market novelty CDs). His assessment: "Jim is a prolific creator of original wit and music, with a dedicated following on the internet, his discovery is still shamefully overlooked by the entertainment industry." 

Music video venues YouTube and now Facebook have indeed been Terr’s sweet spots, with over 1.7 million views of his videos on YouTube and Facebook views of as many as 142,000 (and almost 6,000 “reposts”) on one song, “Jimmy, Come on In,” a tribute to then-ailing President Jimmy Carter.


History and genealogy – theater, music & film:
Terr’s grandparents came from Kiev,Vitebsk, Odessa, and Rostov-na-Don. Being involved in both acting and music, Terr was startled to discover a few years ago that his great-grandfather, Jacob Terr, was a playwright in the Yiddish theater in New York City, collaborating with the famous Boris Thomashefsky. ((http://www.museumoffamilyhistory.com/yt/lex/T/thomashefsky-boris.htm  )) He was even more surprised to find out several years ago that he had three great-uncles named Terr active in Hollywood acting and music from the 1920s through 60s. “Clearly it’s in my blood,” he says in understatement.

In 1982 Terr made a goodwill trip across the Soviet Union to take 3-D (“stereo” / “View Master”) photos of Soviet people, to show in the USA. The project got some very high-profile views and kudos. (http://bluecanyonproductions.com/USSR.html )

He recalls meeting with a large family in Tbilisi, Georgian SSR, who listened raptly to several of his songs even though they did not understand the words. “It knocks here,” one of them explained, indicating the heart, and giving Terr an early hint that his music had some deeper communicative element.
