Regarding Water Supplies . . .

See entire article

At some point, and it is only rational to predict this, freshwater supplies will be completely tapped out. In fact, demand is already outstripping supply as these latest statistics from Blue Gold attest:

Groundwater extractions exceed the recharge rate in the Arabian peninsula by nearly three times.
Seawater intrusion and agricultural runoff have contaminated 13 percent of Israel's coastal aquifers
Non-recharging aquifers in Africa are being depleted at a rate of 10 billion cubic meters per year
India is experiencing falling water tables throughout the entire country
Eight regions in China are subject to aquifer overdraft and Beijing's water table has fallen 37 meters during the past four decades
Mexico city pumps out its aquifers at a rate 50 to 80 percent greater than they recharge, prompting experts to predict that the city will run dry in the next decade

Extractions from the 800-mile wide High Plains Ogallala aquifer in the U.S. exceed the recharge rate by eight times, and finally "21 percent of irrigation is achieved by pumping ground water at rates that exceed the recharge rate."

Exploding executive pay: justified by merit?

(See complete article -- and note that these figures are only up to 1995. Guess how much it's gone up since then...)

In the last 20 years, total pay and compensation of America's top executives have been exploding. Graef Crystal is undoubtedly the United States' most renowned expert on this phenomenon, and his book, In Search of Excess: The Overcompensation of American Executives, commonly serves as the starting point for all debate. (8)

The statistics Crystal reports are not in dispute. Among the 500 biggest firms in the U.S., average CEO compensation in 1975 was 41 times what an average worker earned. By 1995, that ratio had soared to 197 times. (Again, are CEOs five times smarter and harder working than they were 20 years ago? Of course not. Many are the same people.) From 1994 to 1995 alone, CEO compensation rose 16 percent, compared to 2.8 percent for workers, which did not even keep pace with inflation. Their stagnating wages cannot be blamed on corporate profits, which rose a healthy 14.8 percent for that year.

Average CEO pay jumps 535% in 1990's
(http://www.allianceibm.org/articles/ceopay.htm)

11/02/00

Growth in executive salaries over the last decade has far exceeded any growth in average worker salaries for the same time period. The gap in compensation between the average U.S. worker and his/her CEO becomes wider and wider each year. Even corporate profits themselves have grown at a much slower rate then executive compensation.

From a recent study:
"A new study reveals growing pay gaps between workers and CEOs and between CEOs and government officials that are increasing inequality and undermining democracy. CEO pay jumped 535% in the 1990s, dwarfing the 297% rise in the S&P 500, 116% rise in corporate profits and 32% increase in average worker pay (not adjusted for inflation). The pay gap between CEOs and the President of the United States has grown from 2:1 to 62:1 since 1960, reports the Institute for Policy Studies and United for a Fair Economy in Executive Excess 2000."

"If average pay for production workers had grown at the same rate as it has for CEOs during this boom, instead of barely outpacing inflation, their 1999 annual earnings would have been $114,035 instead of $23,753. If the minimum wage had risen as fast as CEO pay, it would now be $24.13 an hour, instead of $5.15."

THE GREATEST STORY EVER TOLD

One bitterly cold night in January of 1935, the mayor (New York Mayor Fiorello LaGuardia) turned up at a night court that served the poorest ward of the city. LaGuardia dismissed the judge for the evening and took over the bench himself. Within a few minutes, a tattered old woman was brought before him, charged with stealing a loaf of bread. She told LaGuardia that her daughter’s husband had deserted her, her daughter was sick, and her two grandchildren were starving.

But the shopkeeper, from whom the bread was stolen, refused to drop the charges. “It’s a real bad neighborhood, Your Honor,” the man told the mayor. “She’s got to be punished to teach other people around here a lesson.”

LaGuardia sighed. He turned to the woman and said, “I’ve got to punish you. The law makes no exceptions. Ten dollars or ten days in jail.” But even as he pronounced sentence, the mayor was already reaching into his pocket. He extracted a bill and tossed it into his famous hat, saying, “Here is the ten dollar fine which I now remit; and furthermore, I am going to fine everyone in this courtroom fifty cents for living in a town where a person has to steal bread so that her grandchildren can eat. Mr. Bailiff, collect the fines and give them to the defendant.”

The following day, New York City newspapers reported that $47.50 was turned over to a bewildered woman who had stolen a loaf of bread to feed her starving grandchildren. Fifty cents of that amount was contributed by the grocery store owner himself, while some seventy petty criminals, people with traffic violations, and New York City policemen, each of whom had just paid fifty cents for the privilege of doing so, gave the mayor a standing ovation.

LETTER FROM A FORMER CABINET SECRETARY

Dear Nephew,

You have asked for my view of the current goings-on, from my vantage point as a former cabinet secretary. I am afraid you may get more than you asked for, and probably considerably darker than you expected.

Yes, you may do what you like with this letter, short of identifying me. I hope that you will understand the arcane rules of the world in which I live, which dictate that I not go entirely public with this, and not judge me too harshly for it. Certain strictures apply to a man almost in his nineties, not entirely disengaged from the battle yet not entirely independent.

On the other hand, my age also gives me a somewhat devil-may-care desire to share what I observe, while I still have eyes to see and a mouth to speak.

You have noticed, I hope, that much of what passes for public discourse in this country is nothing of the sort, but rather a sort of fear-based game of "dodge-em." On the congressional level, for instance, a remarkable number of votes are dictated by a fear of how one's vote may be misrepresented in an opponent's speech or negative campaign ad, whether next week or next year. Therefore you have the strange phenomenon of the House and Senate voting to support the very personal battle plans of a war-obsessed president in spite of overwhelming public opposition to it, as measured by constituent mail and phone calls.

In other words, it is less important that I vote as my constituents wish me to vote, or even as my conscience and senses dictate, than that I vote in such a way that my opponent be unable, in that sort of snarling, grainy, melodramatic campaign ad that has become so common, to accuse me of voting against "American security" or "giving comfort to a dictator who supports terrorism and threatens Americans," or however the charge will undoubtedly be flung in forthcoming campaign commercials.

Which brings us to the dictator in question. Certainly an unpleasant man who makes life miserable for his own people, and who could hardly be replaced by anyone worse. But the American administration's sudden fixation on him, on the urgency of his removal at any cost, would be more credible if he were not seemingly picked almost at random from any number of equally heinous dictators.

The strongest charges made to portray him as some sort of immediate threat to US interests are that he has slaughtered his own people (yes, with US backing), that he has attacked his neighbors (yes, with US encouragement), that he has developed weapons of mass destruction (probably, and what makes his any more threatening than those of any other country, including the US?), that he unstable and possibly insane (no evidence), and that he supports worldwide terrorism (no evidence).

More to the point, President Bush has complained that Saddam "tried to kill my dad." Perhaps -- and of course I'm glad he didn't succeed -- but who could blame him for trying, after being tricked into a war which killed hundreds of thousands of Iraqis at the time and hundreds of thousands since, with idiotic sanctions supposedly intended to punish and oust Saddam, but which have only strengthened him and certainly not deprived him of one calorie of nourishment nor one iota of personal comfort.

I don't blame Mr. Bush for personally wishing to complete the job his father left undone (as he probably views it) and to avenge the attempt on his father's life, but the fact that he and his administration (consisting almost entirely, as you point out, of "chicken hawks" who were too busy or important to serve in the military themselves), have been able to sell the American public on sacrificing a hundred billion dollars and who-knows-how-many American and Iraqi lives on such a personal vendetta, on such a cowboy adventure, is the sad part of this story. Are people that bored and gullible? Evidently so.

Of course the thing that makes Saddam the focus of so much wrath, so enthusiastically joined by the more impressionable and trusting members of the American public, is that he is sitting atop the world's largest remaining reserves of crude oil. The administration has openly admitted its designs on this oil, without significantly damaging its case and its credibility with its more worshipful supporters in the American public. A public who would, in fact, probably support invading Iraq simply and honestly to secure a few more years worth of fuel for their cars, if the question were put more directly. But we maintain this pretense of self-righteousness, self-defense, etc., because it better fits our self-image.

As for the practicalities, it seems obvious that a dictator simply sitting on a probable store of hideous chemical and biological weapons is less threatening than a dictator with his back to the wall, under military assault, likely to discharge these grisly weapons on advancing American troops, tens of thousand of whom are already suffering from the effects of these weapons in the last Gulf war, and probably from the effects of American DU (depleted uranium) anti-tank rounds as well. Certainly the U.N. should strongly assert its right to inspect and destroy these weapons, as agreed under terms of the Iraqi surrender, but how does this translate into America getting itself whipped up for an immediate, urgent, full-scale war, with all the tragedy, horror and phenomenal expense which inevitably attend?

I don’t want to entirely blame the American public for their gullibility in this, without pointing to the two factors which strongly enable this perversity:

First, a rabidly right-wing punditry, most notably on television and particularly on radio, who have succeeded in dominating the debate in those arenas and convincing large segments of the public that anyone (including journalists and news media) who doesn't back the president in this and other matters is a priori an anti-American, a socialist, or both. When you've got your opponents so successfully painted as ignorant traitors, as agents of numerous evil hidden agendas, dialogue is impossible and alternative sources of news and opinion are so threatening as to be avoided.

And certainly this president himself is an ideologue who generally admits no input beyond his own corporate and theological underpinnings. Although he occasionally consents to listen in that pained, bored sort of way when appearances require it, he largely shares this us-against-them attitude about people who might potentially give him some valuable input.

The second "enabling factor" is a press corps largely unwilling to question the inconsistencies and nonsensical pronouncements of public officials, to ask the many obvious unanswered questions. But would you, as a reporter, wish to lose your access to the highly-placed source and the occasional, usually anonymous and self-serving comment, by being a pain in the ass? Would you risk offending a reader or viewer who you and your boss have been convinced is unquestioningly in support of the administration and its conservative, pro-corporate agenda?

Would you, in other words, be willing to risk your job as a journalist by acting as a journalist is supposed to act, has the obligation and privilege to act? Or perhaps we incorrectly assume the press corps consists of people any more discerning and curious and intelligent than the average person in the first place.

Finally, the public themselves: Lavishly entertained but intellectually emasculated and powerless, they have sadly been convinced that this administration, composed largely of corporate CEOs and lobbyists, every day actively cutting labor and environmental and consumer safeguards and rights, flaunting American and international law, supporting a minimum wage that ensures that a family lives below the officially-defined poverty level, having taken us from a substantial budget surplus to a huge deficit in only two years and likely to double that deficit, attending unfailingly to the needs of their corporate partners and little else -- that these Keystone cops who have mangled almost everything they've touched and driven the American economy into the ground, are still somehow the knights in shining armor, the rescuers of American family, economy and culture! It is a modern miracle of media manipulation of which Joseph Goebbels would have been envious.

When a German official, in a moment of candor, compares this administration's attempt to use a foreign war to divert attention from its domestic failings, to the efforts of Hitler to do the same, it is greeted by shock, outrage and "incomprehension" by the administration. Sadly, those protestations are probably sincere. Hitler, at least, was conscious of his manipulations. The current crew is less introspective and more self-righteous, and probably unaware of what they are doing to ensure their own grasp on power.

Nor do I see much hope for improvement. We are talking, after all, about a public who is unruffled by revelations that they were suckered into the Gulf War by images of Iraqi soldiers ripping babies from incubators and by alleged satellite photos of Iraqi troops massing on the Kuwaiti border -- images entirely manufactured for public consumption by a P.R. firm! And that they are being suckered into a hundred-billion-dollar "Star Wars" program by rigged missile tests. And that war games handily won by a clever, low-tech general are ignored as though they never happened, by a military who wishes to believe that their high-tech approach will always win.

A public who doesn't care about being so manipulated, then or now, by numerous other "Gulf of Tonkin" manufactured events with most serious and tragic global consequences, I call powerless and emasculated. This is a public who sees no problem with being governed by a congress which grants themselves the most lavish health care and retirement system imaginable, while largely letting the peasants drown when it comes to these matters. A public who largely does not understand the simple sixth-grade concept that legal safeguards which protect the rights of alleged terrorists, also protect themselves if wrongly accused.

And what does this tell you: A senate hopeful in New Mexico announces that in the past five years her long-entrenched opponent has voted consistently to keep the minimum wage at poverty level, while in the same period raising his own salary by over 300 per cent! My reports are that this remarkable example of hypocrisy has gotten little press, and even less interest from voters. Do you wonder that I have a pessimistic view of a public so accustomed to taking what gruel they are given, led by a disingenuous administration with an uncommon level of media savvy?

My only consolation, my only glimmer of hope, is that the American people -- albeit by a very tiny margin -- did not actually vote this crew into office. May we survive them to see a better day!

My love to you and to your mother.
