DOCTOR LAURA: A MIXED BLESSING
© By Jim Terr 2000

Unlike many of Dr. Laura Schlessinger's most persistent critics, I consider her to be a real treasure in many respects-insightful, intuitive and often compassionate in dealing with many of her callers' problems and "moral dilemmas." Her priorities and her concerns are in line with many of my own, and I dare I say I've even learned a few things from her!

In some ways, however, I consider Dr. Laura to be a menace, on a par with some of the people and trends which she demonizes daily on her radio and TV programs.

Take her attitude on gays. The past year's flood of letters, editorials, protests and ad campaigns directed at Dr. Laura's "hatred" of gays is generally overblown in one respect: I have listened to Dr. Laura for several hours a week for the past couple of years, and I would not describe her attitude toward homosexuals as one of "hate." She claims-and I'll take her at her word-to know and respect many gays.

But I've also heard her describe the urge to homosexuality as a "mistake," a biological "error" and "deviance," and this is what has enraged many gay individuals and organizations. Dr. Laura has, in her defense, tried to distinguish between the individual and the behavior, between the sinner and the sin. I have heard her say many times that gays are okay with her, as long as they don't actually practice homosexuality.

This is like saying that I appreciate Dr. Laura as a person, as long as she doesn't actually exercise her overpowering urge to share her wisdom on the radio. That is clearly Dr. Laura's most salient talent, her most basic instinct, and to deny her this outlet would be to deny her most natural source of self-expression and satisfaction-not to mention her livelihood.

When pressed, Dr. Laura justifies her hard stance on gay abstention by quoting Scripture (selectively, of course, as most Scripture-quoters seem to do), and by a sort of converse logic: the basic, Biblically-ordained relationship is that of man and woman, so that the earth may be populated, and according to Dr. Laura any deviation from that is not only anti-Biblical and evil but counter-productive as well. Has anyone told Dr. Laura that the earth currently has upwards of six billion inhabitants, mostly overcrowded and underfed, and that populating the earth is no longer the same imperative it was 4,000 years ago? Does Dr. Laura condemn single people, or married heterosexual couples who have chosen not to produce children? No, she does not.

Then where does she get off denying pleasure and fulfillment to those gay couples who wish to enjoy their lives and mind their own business, as Dr. Laura is privileged to do? How does her often-stated (and often-demonstrated) sense of compassion square with this particular crusade of hers? Why are homosexuals singled out for torment by this woman with so much good sense and compassion in so many other areas? Why does she interpret every attempt by gays to gain the same rights as everyone else, to be an "anti-family" conspiracy?

Dr. Laura has stated that attacks on her serve only to increase her popularity. To the extent that such attacks are inflated and inaccurate, she is probably right, since she flaunts them as examples of how she is unfairly persecuted by the press and the public.

But on this one issue, if she ever decides to put as much energy into introspection as she currently puts into attack and counter-attack, she may find that she owes her listeners-especially the gays whom she has hurt directly and indirectly-a sincere apology.

Jim Terr of Santa Fe, NM, is the author of "Letters to Doctor Laura, and Other Struggles Against Demagoguery and Fundamentalism" (http://www.bluecanyonproductions.com/letters.html). (If you wish to use a photo of the writer, you can download one from www.bluecanyonproductions.com/photo).


COURAGE, CHARACTER and APPLE PIE
©2000 Jim Terr (740 words)

Each of our two leading contenders for the presidency would like us to think that they have more character than the other, more courage, a greater portion of trustworthiness and integrity. Aside from the self-puffery that this represents, which only a few years ago would have been considered immodest, what lies behind these claims to moral superiority?

On the part of Governor Bush, these references to his superior morality are code words meant to imply that there will be no extra-marital dalliance in his White House, no unacknowledged fundraisers in Buddhist temples, and none of the general dishonesty and disingenuousness that hard-core conservatives agree is the key operating principle of the Clinton-Gore Administration.

On the part of Vice President Gore, any hints at higher ground might be taken as references to Bush's blatant corporate cronyism in his choice of advisors, appointed boards and running mate, not to mention his general fealty to the wealthy.

Bush's tragically absurd certainty about the guilt of everyone executed in Texas, both men's devotion to The Government/Business Revolving Door and The Big Spin Machine-the departures from Principle are nearly endless.

But where is the real courage, character and integrity? Which of these candidates has the courage to question pumping up a military budget that already comprises 51% of our federal expenditures-a budget already $41 billion over what the Pentagon requested for the past six years--in a time of relative peace?** And how about an extra $60 billion for a "Star Wars" system that most scientists and household tinkerers agree would never work, would only make the Russians feel more edgy, and which would be directed only at a country stupid enough to send us a missile with their return address on it rather than just bringing in an atomic weapon in a suitcase?

Where is the character in the fact that neither of these candidates had the decency-or even the political savvy-to ask for prayers for the crewmen of the Russian submarine "Kursk" when the rest of the country was agonizing over their fate? Or would that have been too politically risky, something that there was insufficient time to hold a focus group about?

Where is the character in Al Gore failing to thank George Bush for participating in the first presidential debate, even after Bush thanked Gore? Where is the courage in both of these candidates' unwillingness to invite Ralph Nader to join in? Where is the commitment to democracy, when polls show consistently that between 60 and 64 per cent of Americans wish to see them debate Nader?

Where is the integrity in failing to "step outside the box" for a moment to address such non-focus-group-tested questions as "If humans do not inhabit the earth in 50 years, what will have been the most likely reason, and what have I done to address it?" Whether the answer is nuclear war, depleted topsoil and water supplies or other environmental devastation-all good possibilities-could we hear a few words about it?

Where is the courage in not taking one moment out of the thousands of hours of self-promotion and chest-beating in their campaigns, to talk about how the starvation, disease and suffering which afflict a great portion of our fellow human beings might be alleviated by this bountiful, creative country, and whether this might contribute more to our spiritual well-being than a perfect record of marital fidelity while in office?

Where is the leadership in both of these gentlemen's unwillingness to call a moratorium on unregulated "soft money" TV campaign ads, the most blatant example of a system so rotten that even they must recognize it? If Rick Lazio and Hillary Clinton can do the same in the media- and money-rich environment of New York, why can't these two do it, too? Are they both willing to admit that they're this crass and this powerless?

But okay, forget about what I think is important, what I think would be some good indicators of courage, character and integrity. I'd ask these candidates (are you listening, Jim Lehrer?): What have you done, on the campaign trail or elsewhere this year, that involves any real courage or self-sacrifice, beyond simple self-aggrandizement and posturing?

In short, where's the courage to say something-anything!-about some of the things that are wrong in our world that can't be blamed on Democrats or Republicans, something that's obvious to the rest of us, something meaningful, something of real personal concern, without being paralyzed by the fear of having it turned into a negative TV sound bite? Do either of these men really have the faith in the American people that they claim to have?

Jim Terr of Santa Fe, NM, is the author of "Letters to Doctor Laura, and Other Struggles against Demagoguery and Fundamentalism" (http://www.bluecanyonproductions.com/letters.html)
