To get right to the point… 

CBS "Evening News" anchor and American icon Dan Rather has a problem. We're stepping forward to help, and we'd like to ask you to do the same. 

Although he makes $7 million a year, Rather is evidently insecure about his role as an independent journalist. As a guest on "Late Night With David Letterman" September 17, 2002, Rather stated: "George Bush is the President. He makes the decisions, and, you know, [I'm] just one American, wherever he wants me to line up, just tell me where. And he’ll make the call." 

Okay, perhaps an understandable bit of sentimental patriotism one week after the disastrous events of September 11, but then, a full eight months later:

 

US media cowed by patriotic fever, says CBS star  

Network news veteran admits national mood caused him to shrink from tough questions on war in Afghanistan  

            (See story excerpt below, or see complete story at www.guardian.co.uk)


Matthew Engel in Washington
Friday May 17, 2002
The Guardian

Dan Rather, the star news anchor for the US television network CBS, said last night that "patriotism run amok" was in danger of trampling the freedom of American journalists to ask tough questions. And he admitted that he had shrunk from taking on the Bush administration over the war on terrorism.

In the weeks after September 11 Rather wore a Stars and Stripes pin in his lapel during his evening news show in an apparent display of total solidarity with the American cause. However, in an interview with BBC's Newsnight, he graphically described the pressures to conform that built up after the attacks on the World Trade Centre and the Pentagon.      

"It is an obscene comparison - you know I am not sure I like it - but you know there was a time in South Africa that people would put flaming tyres around people's necks if they dissented. And in some ways the fear is that you will be necklaced here, you will have a flaming tyre of lack of patriotism put around your neck," he said. "Now it is that fear that keeps journalists from asking the toughest of the tough questions."

 

Perhaps journalists who make tiny fraction of Rather's salary might be forgiven for shrinking from the tough, sometimes obvious questions, and asking instead the fawning question of their highly-placed sources, in order to protect their access to "off-the-record" information and the occasional exclusive interview, but shouldn't Rather feel secure in his position as an independent journalist, at $7 million a year? 

Clearly he doesn't, and that's why we're requesting that in order to restore Rather's self-confidence and independence, and make him once again an inspiration for younger and less well-paid journalists, that you send Rather a check! Send to:

Dan Rather
c/o CBS Evening News

New York, NY 10019. 

(Do not send cash). He'll thank you for it, and we thank you for it. 

You might also enclose one of your favorite quotes on the independence of the press, from a traditional source like Bartlett's Familiar Quotations or any other source, or any related quote such as this from Benjamin Franklin: "Those that would sacrifice their freedom for safety will find they inherit neither." 

We appreciate all Americans pulling together in this effort to support one of our own. 


HOW FAR WE'VE FALLEN… 
Bill Clinton and Amy Goodman

A most startling demonstration of how out-of-touch our leaders are, how accustomed they've become to being asked powder-puff questions by journalists, occurred on Election Day of 2000, when President Bill Clinton telephoned Amy Goodman, host of the Democracy Now! radio program, for a two-minute interview that turned into a 30-minute wrangle.  

Clinton's defensiveness at being asked some tough, direct questions— for a change—was most revealing. Judge for yourself whether Amy did anything more than a journalist should do, whether she was impolite and aggressive, as Clinton repeatedly accused her of being, by listening to this remarkable exchange at http://www.webactive.com. For her trouble, Amy was subsequently threatened by a White House official with total loss of access! 

Incidentally, isn't it interesting that the supposedly far-left Democracy Now! is one of the few programs anywhere on radio (right, left or in between) which consistently invites representatives of all sides of an issue—including industry and administration officials—to participate in debates in order to produce a full, balanced picture on any given issue?
