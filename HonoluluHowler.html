Trump Critic Denies Hating Trump

   Special to The Houston Howler, April 13, 2025

 Jim Terr's YouTube channel boasts almost three million views, and many of his recent videos are about the shortcomings of Donald Trump - some of them quite dire and alarming. For instance, "Reichstag Fire" flat-out predicts that Trump will use some pretense for a national emergency to declare martial law and cancel the upcoming elections - just as Hitler did.

Still, the New Mexico satirist declares in a recent fundraising post, "I don't hate Trump. I consider him, like Hitler and Stalin and other megalomaniacs, to be a damaged personality who has unfortunately gotten into a position of power where he can do a lot of damage, as we can see every day... I think he's still traumatized by his recent brush with imprisonment, and I think that's making him even more vicious than he already was."

Satirist Jim Terr 

In numerous songs and essays, Terr has no hesitation in comparing Trump to the German dictator, in terms of his desire for full control of the country. "I think it's obvious that's his intention, and that he's put together a like-minded team who's very supportive of that, and the best use of my talents is to try to put a wedge between him and his supporters by creating satire that might reduce the enchantment he casts over his cult."


As a nationally-broadcast songwriter, jingle producer, and satirist, Terr has gained many kudos, but he regrets that he has yet to crack the "viral" video world of TikTok, X, and Instagram -- despite having as many as several hundred thousand views on some of his videos. "I think I need to hire a young consultant who knows that world better than I do," he says.


In addition to getting random donations for his work on occasional GoFundMe projects, he also has a very small base of Patreon supporters who "help me pay the bills," he says. His current project includes sending "wake-up" postcards to the GOP congresspeople who support Trump, something he has done before.


For a couple of award-winning documentaries he has produced, he has interviewed New Mexico Holocaust survivors and World War Two veterans, and for other videos he has interviewed prominent Trump critic (and ex-staff-member) Anthony Scaramucci, as well as humorist Dave Barry, NPR's Scott Simon, James Fallows, Tony Hillerman, Arianna Huffington, Charles Osgood, Valerie Plame, Mark Rudd, Ali MacGraw, Thom Hartmann and others.

"But I'd like to interview Donald Trump if I ever had a chance," he says. "Not to give him more of a platform, but to try to draw out some interesting insights into this malignant character -- and perhaps even have a good influence on him."

 You've got to give one thing to Terr, who is cited in Wikipedia as both a song satirist and a Presidential candidate: he's not modest! 

-J.S.

The surprising reason the planets align

Our friend and correspondent in Las Vegas, New Mexico, at the center of the recent disastrous forest fires and normally a haven for clear-sky stargazing when there are no fires, muses about the many articles he's seen about the current alignment of the planets.

He says that of the 15 or 20 articles he's seen on the subject, not one explains why the planets always appear to be in alignment when they are visible in the night sky. (Though he confesses he himself only learned this a couple years ago).

It's because the planets (including Earth) are all more or less on the same plane, like marbles on a tabletop, so that if you're looking from that same plane (which we are, looking from Earth), any or all of the others will appear to be in a line, just as the marbles would if you look at them from the edge of the tabletop.

Kind of interesting, kind of simple, yet still mind blowing! Leaving only the mystery of why this is never mentioned in any news reports. Is it ignorance, lack of curiosity, or preferring a magical, sensational story?

                   -Editor.

Visiting filmmaker sheds little light on recent Rust shooting incident

A New Mexico filmmaker with no particular expertise and nothing to add to the conversation, has weighed in on the tragic shooting event on the set of the Alec Baldwin film, Rust, in Santa Fe.

Jim Terr, who has made his mark in foundation-funded documentaries more so than feature films, spoke to a Howler reporter over a bowl of Zip Min at Zippy's Makiki this week, offering his "admittedly uninformed" thoughts on the recent tragic shooting.

"First, I don't know the difference between a prop gun and a real gun, between a 'live round,' and a 'blank,'" Terr said. "And I understand it even less than I did before this happened and I read all the commentaries," he added.

"I've only been involved with one gun on one project," he said, referring to his award-winning short, The Wrong Guy, which he directed in 2001. "The brain-splattering effect turned out so realistically that I had to warn the stars, who were boyfriend and girlfriend, before showing it to them. They appreciated that."

"The editor, Ryan Denmark, who went on to edit for Spike Lee, even added a few frames of subliminal expanding red to the bullet hole, making it even more disturbing," Terr added, admitting that none of this sheds any light on the Santa Fe incident.

"My other big project, The Brill Building Movie, involved no firearms whatsoever, and I can attest that that did not detract at all from the appeal of the film. Clearly, there was carelessness on the Rust set with regard to firearms and ammunition, and maybe even some evil intent if somebody threw some real lead into the mix of ammo."

"But again, what do I know?" Terr added. "I don't even recall how we handled the gun used in The Wrong Guy, though I vaguely remember the actor checking the gun carefully before shooting at his (actual) girlfriend. Sounds like good policy."

"It's a real shame," Terr added, again stating the obvious. "I hope this incident greatly reduces the chances of such a thing happening again, on another film set, for a long, long time."
The Brill Building Movie is the semi-fictional telling of Terr's songwriting career, featuring excerpts of many of his songs, with commentary by British country music publicist and journalist Tony Byworth, co-author of The Billboard Encyclopedia of Country Music.

It stars ex-spy Valerie Plame, with cameos by author Tony Hillerman, former New Mexico Governor Bill Richardson, NPR's Scott Simon, humorist Dave Barry, journalists James Fallows and Hodding Carter III and other celebrities, and "a cast of hundred." It also features Suhail Dabbach, the memorable "Man in Black" from 2010 Oscar Best Picture The Hurt Locker and star of the recent acclaimed film, Mosul.

 Terr says he is investigating offering the film -- including the many song properties therein -- as an NFT (nonfungible token) bitcoin investment.
