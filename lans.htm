Woman's Food Has Authentic Flavor
   July 25, 2006 Albuquerque Journal
   Section: Business Page: S1
   KIERA HAY Journal Staff Writer

At Lan's Vietnamese Cuisine on Don Gaspar Avenue, owner Lan thi Tran does things her own way. She operates only five hours a day, provides seating for just 15 people and doesn't advertise beyond a few fliers. Since her restaurant opened on July 10, however, it has already been attracting patrons interested in the exotic, all-natural Asian fare on hand.

"People are coming to this place … I don't know how they've found me so quick," said Tran, a Vietnamese native.

"We want to be the best kept secret in Santa Fe, until word gets out," co-owner Clint Demmon added, laughing.

Tran, 40, grew up in a family of nine children. Her father, who broke his back while picking up a rice bag delivered by an American aid organization, was unable to work. Her mother, finding it impossible to support nine children and an ailing husband, sent Tran and four other siblings to live with their grandmother in a remote rural village.

There was no electricity or running water in the grandmother's small hut, situated two hours from the nearest market. Tran and her family grew or raised all their own food, trading with neighbors for items they didn't have.

There wasn't always a lot to go around, however. Tran said she remembers days when each member of the family would have only half a bowl of rice, as well as some vegetables, to last them through the day. She would add water and soy sauce to the food in an attempt to make it "coat" her stomach.

Tran said she and her siblings would wake each morning at 5 or 6 a.m. and work all day in the family's garden. It wasn't until evening, after dinner, that the children would have a chance to play.

There wasn't money for school, and Tran said she completed only three years of formal education, something she regrets.

She moved to Santa Fe several years ago after falling in love with, and subsequently marrying, a New Mexican who was visiting Vietnam.

When she came to America, she left two small children behind and said homesickness, combined with adjusting to the high altitude, left her so ill her weight dropped to 70 pounds. She later had to cope with the failure of her marriage.

Tran's two children still live in Vietnam, but she sends them about $300 a month, which covers their living expenses and education. She said she talks to them every three days.         >>>

 

   (continued from previous column)

When cooking at Lan's, Tran said she tries to emulate the food she ate while growing up. She looks to her grandmother, who is 95, as a culinary and health inspiration. "I learned a lot from her," she said.

Tran creates Lan's menus with food bought from Vietnam and American Asian markets. She also dips into her own garden.

"I'm so happy God gave me a skill like this (cooking), so I can make a living," she said. Nothing on the menu is over $8.50, although Lan's offers several unique items. One of the specialties is cao lau, a dish made with rice noodles and served in the city of Hoi An.

Yet even as she gains success as an American businesswoman, Tran's life remains impacted by her experiences in Vietnam.

She wants her children to attend college and beamed with pride after mentioning that her oldest daughter would begin university in the fall.

She also said she can't pass a beggar without handing over whatever money she has in her pocket.

"We live here. We have to take care of each other. We have to share," she said.

(From Santa Fe New Mexican
      Pasatiempo, September 15, 2006)

Of two new Vietnamese restaurants in Santa Fe, Lan's Vietnamese Cuisine has a wonderfully authentic, family-kitchen feel; The pho, won tons, curried vegetarian soups, and an unusual coconut flan stand out at Lan's.

We tried the egg rolls, My Quang chicken (also available in a tofu variation), won tons, Vietnamese crepes, and Ca Vi, a vegetarian curry soup. Hunter, age 10, liked the won tons and the My Quang chicken with its wide, flat noodles. Ethan, 9, didn't like anything. My daughter raved about the steamed won tons with their delicate flavors of chicken and herbs, garlic, and sweet-and-sour papaya.

Seasoned with mint, cilantro, and sweet-and-sour papaya, [Lan Tranh]'s pho was superior -- well worth its reputation as a Vietnamese specialty. The curried vegetarian soup contained both soy protein and tofu, noodles, mint, and cilantro. The waitress explained that what she was serving was a little different from the description on the menu; the soup can vary daily depending on the ingredients on hand.
